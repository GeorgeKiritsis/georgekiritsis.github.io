Designing Ikaros: a Cockpit-Class Flight Data Recorder

2026 · ~13 min read

The Tecnam P2002-JF is the primary trainer at the Hellenic Air Force Academy, and like most light training aircraft it carries no flight-data recorder. There is no black box, no way to reconstruct what a cadet actually did on a sortie beyond the instructor's memory and a paper debrief. Ikaros is my answer to that gap: a self-contained recorder that captures attitude, position, and dynamics at high rate onto a microSD card, then hands that data to the Skyris replay engine for analysis.

The constraints were real. It had to be small and light enough to sit in a trainer cockpit, run on cockpit power without a host computer, survive vibration, and produce data faithful enough that a replay would match what the pilot flew. This note is about the engineering underneath that: the board, the sensor timing, the attitude estimation, and the deliberately boring file format that ties it to the rest of the system.

The board

At the centre is an STM32F405RGT6, an ARM Cortex-M4 running at 168 MHz with a hardware floating-point unit, 1 MB of flash, and 192 KB of SRAM. The FPU is not a luxury here; the attitude filter runs hundreds of times a second and is dense with floating-point work, and doing that in software emulation would blow the timing budget. Around it sit three sensors and two peripherals, each chosen for a specific job.

The BMI088 in particular is an aviation-minded choice: it is designed for high-vibration environments, with the accelerometer and gyroscope on separate dies to reject cross-axis vibration coupling, which is exactly the failure mode a piston trainer produces. The whole system is bare-metal C on the STM32 HAL, with no operating system. A cooperative scheduler over hardware timers was the deliberate alternative to an RTOS: the timing requirements are fixed and known, so a preemptive kernel would add complexity and jitter without buying anything.

Sampling: different sensors, different rates

The sensors do not all deserve the same attention. The gyroscope and accelerometer change fast and drive the attitude filter, so they are read at 400 Hz. The magnetometer only corrects slow heading drift, so 50 Hz is plenty. The barometer is slower still. Everything is orchestrated by a 1 ms timer tick that acts as the system heartbeat, with each task claiming its slot by a simple counter.

Each sensor also gets its raw output conditioned. The BMI088 registers arrive as two bytes that must be reassembled and sign-extended before scaling to physical units, and every channel passes through a lightweight per-channel Kalman filter to suppress the high-frequency noise a piston engine injects into everything bolted to the airframe:

// reassemble a signed 16-bit sample from two SPI bytes, then scale
int16_t raw = (int16_t)((hi << 8) | lo);
float   acc = (float)raw * ACC_RANGE / 32768.0f;   // e.g. +/- 6 g

Attitude estimation with Mahony AHRS

No single sensor knows which way the aircraft is pointing. The gyroscope measures angular rate, which integrates into orientation but drifts without bound. The accelerometer senses gravity, giving an absolute pitch and roll reference, but is corrupted by every maneuver. The magnetometer senses magnetic north for heading, but is noisy and disturbed by nearby metal and current. Sensor fusion is the art of combining them so each covers the others' weaknesses, and Ikaros uses a Mahony AHRS filter for it.

Mahony was the right filter for this hardware over the more famous alternative, the Extended Kalman Filter. An EKF is more accurate in principle but carries a heavy per-step cost of matrix inversions and Jacobian evaluations. Mahony achieves comparable accuracy for this application at a fraction of the compute, which is what lets it close the loop at 400 Hz on a microcontroller. The gains were tuned empirically: Kp = 2.0 sets how aggressively the absolute references pull the estimate, and a small Ki = 0.005 lets the filter learn and cancel the gyroscope's slow bias without chasing noise.

The measured effect of the integral term is stark. With the accelerometer and magnetometer correction disabled, heading drifts about 4.8 degrees per minute of pure gyro integration. With the full filter running, yaw error stays under 0.3 degrees in straight-and-level flight and around 1.1 degrees through sustained turns, where centripetal acceleration briefly contaminates the gravity reference. That is faithful enough that the replayed attitude in Skyris matches what the pilot flew.

The file format is the interface contract

Ikaros writes to the microSD card as plain CSV through the FatFs library, at 100 Hz, with 21 fields per row: timestamp, raw and filtered IMU, quaternion and Euler attitude, barometric altitude, and GPS position, speed, and course. There is nothing clever about the format, and that is the point. CSV is the deliberately boring, universal contract between the recorder and every downstream consumer.

Two engineering details keep the logging honest. Writes are buffered and flushed to the card every 100 samples rather than every row, because a raw SD write can stall unpredictably for 5 to 20 ms, and flushing on every sample would jam the fast sensor loop. Batching amortises that cost. And on the read side, Skyris does not assume a fixed column order; it parses the header and maps names to indices, which is what makes the CSV a durable contract rather than a brittle coincidence of field positions.

What the whole thing adds up to

Ikaros turns an aircraft with no instrumentation into one that records a complete, replayable account of every sortie, for the cost of a small custom board and a microSD card. The engineering that matters is not any single exotic part; it is the set of proportionate choices. SPI over I2C for noise immunity. A cooperative scheduler over an RTOS because the timing is known. Mahony over an EKF because it fits the silicon. Batched writes because SD cards stall. And a plain-text format because the most robust interface between two systems you control is the one with the fewest secrets.

Paired with the Skyris engine and the anomaly-detection pipeline, it closes the loop from raw motion in the cockpit to an automated, phase-aware grade an instructor can act on. The hardware is the first link in that chain, and everything downstream is only as trustworthy as the data this board writes.
